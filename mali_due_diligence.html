Section I

Background: Mali's Gold Sector

Gold represents the backbone of Mali's economy — accounting for roughly 80% of total exports and historically between five and ten percent of GDP. The Ministry of Mines estimates the country holds 881 metric tons of gold deposits, with approximately 300 artisanal mining sites and over two million people — more than ten percent of the population — dependent on mining for their livelihood.

The industry is split between large-scale industrial operations dominated by multinationals — Canada's Barrick Mining, Australia's Resolute Mining, and Canada's B2Gold — and a vast artisanal and small-scale sector operating largely outside formal regulatory oversight. The OECD has identified artisanal gold from the Sahel as especially vulnerable to illicit financing, armed groups, and money laundering.

The political landscape shifted dramatically after military coups in August 2020 and May 2021 installed Colonel Assimi GoïtaInterim president of Mali since the 2021 coup. Head of the Comité National pour le Salut du Peuple (CNSP). Faces ECOWAS sanctions. as interim president. Cut off from Western development aid, the junta turned to the mining sector as its primary revenue source.

In August 2023, the government enacted a revised Mining Code (Law No. 2023-040)Raised the state's mandatory equity share in new mining projects to 35%. Dramatically increased fiscal obligations for foreign operators. Retroactive application disputed by companies., significantly raising the state's share in mining projects and increasing fiscal obligations on foreign operators. This is part of a wider wave of resource nationalism across the Sahel — Niger, Burkina Faso, Senegal, and Ivory Coast have all enacted similar measures.

"Mali represents the strongest example in a larger historical pattern of resource nationalism — a trend that is reshaping how foreign capital accesses African extractive industries."

Case Study: The Barrick–Mali Dispute

The confrontation between Barrick Mining Corporation (NYSE: GOLD) and the Government of Mali over the Loulo-Gounkoto gold complexLocated in southwestern Mali near the Senegalese border. In production since 2005. ~8,000 workers, ~97% Malian nationals. Barrick owns 80%, Malian state 20%. Total investment: $10B+. is one of the most consequential mining disputes in recent African history. Barrick acquired the complex in 2019 via its purchase of Randgold Resources and had invested over ten billion dollars in Mali.

Parallel Case
Resolute Mining: Similar Dispute

In November 2024, Resolute Mining CEO Terence Holohan was detained in Bamako. The company paid $160 million to resolve a parallel tax dispute — confirming the Barrick case is not an isolated incident but a pattern of sovereign enforcement.

Section III

Sanctions Risk: Wagner / Africa Corps

Beyond sovereign risk, Mali's gold sector presents a unique compliance exposure: OFAC-designatedOffice of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) is a division of the U.S. Treasury that administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions. Doing business with designated entities can expose firms to significant civil and criminal liability. Russian paramilitary actors are directly embedded in the gold supply chain. The Wagner Group, rebranded under Russia's Ministry of Defense as Africa Corps, maintains an operational presence in Mali's artisanal mining sector.

Russian mercenaries entered Mali in 2021–2022. At peak, approximately 2,000 Wagner personnel were deployed; ~1,000 remained as of early 2024. The Malian government reportedly pays ~$10.8 million per month for services, with payment partially made in gold. Wagner captured at least four artisanal mining sites — including Intahaka, Mali's largest artisanal mine in the Gao region — with Malian military assistance.

Section IV

Assessment & Recommendations

Mali's gold sector represents a convergence of sovereign, sanctions, reputational, and operational risk at levels that are among the highest in the global mining industry. The Barrick case demonstrates that even the most established operators, backed by decades of investment and formal contractual protections, are not immune to asset seizure, executive detention, or coerced renegotiation. The concurrent presence of OFAC-designated paramilitary actors in the artisanal supply chain adds a distinct and serious compliance dimension.

01
Conduct Enhanced Counterparty Screening
Screen all direct and indirect counterparties, financial instruments, and supply chain links against OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals list. Particular attention to Wagner/Africa Corps-affiliated entities and the UAE transshipment corridor (Dubai-based intermediaries).
Monitor Regulatory Developments
02
Monitor Regulatory Developments
The Loulo-Gounkoto mining permit expires February 2026. Permit renewal terms under the 2023 Mining Code remain highly uncertain. Barrick's post-settlement operational status will serve as a key leading indicator of junta attitudes toward foreign investment.
03
Assess Regional Portfolio Risk
Sahel resource nationalism is not Mali-specific. Niger, Burkina Faso, Senegal, and Ivory Coast have all enacted significant interventions. Exposure to any single Sahelian jurisdiction may signal broader, systemic portfolio risk across the region.
04
Engage Linguistic & Cultural Expertise
A significant portion of Mali's information environment operates in Bambara and French. Critical OSINT — including community-level disputes, local governance conflicts, and informal mining agreements — is inaccessible through English-language monitoring alone. Cultural context (e.g., sanankɛya governance) also shapes how disputes develop and resolve.
