Peer-Reviewed Conference Presentation · LAK'25

Bumpy Journey: Using Learning Analytics to Understand Undergraduate CS Gateway Course Performance and Major Switch Decisions

Chen, L.K., Ngo, S., Coulibaly, S., Talwar, A., Vyas, N., & Konkobo, K. (2025). Companion Proceedings of the 15th International Conference on Learning Analytics & Knowledge (LAK'25). March 2025.

This study used learning analytics to examine why students leave Computer Science majors at a minority-serving institution. The team built predictive models to forecast whether students would pass CS gateway courses, and analyzed whether major switches were "forced" (due to failing required courses) or "voluntary" (chosen despite not failing). The goal was to inform more equitable advising and curriculum design for underrepresented students in computing.

1 A logistic regression model using first-term GPA and math course grades achieved an AUC of 0.93, correctly identifying 90% of students who would eventually pass gateway courses.
2 Of 693 students who switched majors, only 28.4% were "forced" out by failing gateway courses. The majority left voluntarily, pointing to belonging and fit as larger drivers than academic failure alone.
3 Female students were more likely to voluntarily leave CS (79%) compared to male students (69%), a pattern consistent across all race groups in the cohort.
4 Students from lower-income backgrounds were more likely to voluntarily leave the CS major, potentially because they lacked the financial flexibility to retake courses and absorb the added time and cost.

// Project 03 Methodology
Mali Gold Due Diligence
Risk Assessment Framework
01
Scoping the Questions
The central question: what sovereign, sanctions, and reputational risks would a U.S. entity face by engaging with actors in Mali's gold supply chain? Secondary questions included which specific companies and officials carry designations, and what historical arbitration disputes reveal about contractual risk.
02
Data Sources
18 primary sources, including U.S. Treasury OFAC designation records, ICSID international arbitration filings, investigative journalism from Reuters and Le Monde, and State Department country risk reports. All sources linked and cited directly in the final deliverable.
03
Risk Classification
Each risk category (sovereign, sanctions, reputational, operational) was scored across a three-tier matrix: Critical, High, and Moderate. Ratings were anchored to specific evidence, not judgment alone.
04
Output
A structured due diligence brief designed to support a compliance or investment team making a go/no-go decision. Formatted to mirror industry-standard third-party risk reports used in federal contracting and international finance.
Key Finding
Sovereign and sanctions risk were rated Critical, driven by active OFAC designations against key figures in the Malian gold sector and the military government's ongoing nationalization of mining contracts. The assessment recommends a full compliance review before any direct or indirect engagement.
